{% blocktrans %}Machu Picchu{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Urban Sector{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The urban sector of Machu Picchu was divided into 3 districts: the Sacred District, the Popular District, and the District of the Priests and the Nobility.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Trapezoidal Architecture{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The doors, windows and wall structures throughout Machu Picchu feature a variety of textures and sizes; though all feature the traditional trapezoidal structure. The trapezoidal structure offered greater strength and protection against collapse during seismic movements, which are common in the region.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Intiwatana{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The Intiwatna rock was the ceremonial center of Machu Picchu and was of great religious significance. Translated as sun (inti) year (wata), Incan astronomers studied the solar year, possibly to determine the solstices and equinoxes.The Incas also may have used Intiwana as a directional landmark to reference magnetic North.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Temple of the Three Windows{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The Temple of the Three Windows is one of the most striking buildings at Machu Picchu. The temple owes its name to the main wall, where there are three large trapezoidal windows and two open spaces. The stonework demonstrates meticulous work; each enormous stone is separated from the other by mere millimeters.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}The Main Temple{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The Main Temple was built in the form of a wayrana, a rectangular structure with only 3 walls. The temple is located north of the sacred plaza, near the Temple of the Three Windows.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Temple of the Sun{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The Temple of the Sun is built in a semi-circular structure following the natural curve of its foundation of existing granite rock. The temple features intricate stone work, including a stone gate with drilled holes and two trapezoidal windows with stone molding.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Plazas of Machu Picchu{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}There are four plazas in Machu Picchu located at different levels, yet they all feature classic Incan architecture in the form of their rectangular shape. The architects linked them together by staircases that were built into the construction of the terraces. The largest of these is the main square, which fulfilled religious and social functions.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}Agricultural Sector{% endblocktrans %} {% blocktrans %}The Agricultural Sector of Machu Picchu is comprised of a series of agricultural irrigation terraces of various sizes and types. These terraces likely performed 2 primary functions: crop growing and protection from water erosion from heavy rains. Five storehouse structures (called “collpas” or “granaries”) are located within the sector.{% endblocktrans %}
